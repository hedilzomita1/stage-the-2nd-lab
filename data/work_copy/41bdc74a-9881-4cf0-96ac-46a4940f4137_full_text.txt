Question #1: My Ikigai 

My Ikigai is translating advanced material science into restorative patient solutions. 

The central theme of my career is "Integration." My research—improving how synthetic implants 
(like PEEK) integrate with living bone—reflects my broader professional desire: to bridge the 
gap between academic discovery and clinical application. My talent lies in understanding the 
complex interface where biology meets engineering (surface chemistry, cell adhesion), a niche 
that the medical device industry (Medtronic, J&J) needs to master to create the next generation 
of implants. 

I can create a lasting impact by ensuring that medical devices are not just "biocompatible" (safe) 
but "bioactive" (healing). To align my career with this, I am ready to transition from the "publish 
or perish" mindset of academia to the "patient-first" and "regulatory-compliant" mindset of 
industry. I am willing to trade the freedom of blue-sky research for the rigor of developing 
marketable, FDA-approved products. 

Question #2: My Ideal Day 

My ideal day balances scientific rigor with strategic application. 

It begins with reviewing technical data—perhaps analyzing surface characterization results 
(XPS or SEM) for a new implant coating. I enjoy being the "expert in the room" whom engineers 
consult when they encounter a biological failure. 

By mid-day, I shift to a cross-functional role. I am in a meeting with the Regulatory Affairs team, 
explaining why a specific chemical modification is safe and effective, helping them draft the 
submission for a new device. I enjoy translating complex science into clear, defensible 
arguments for stakeholders. The day ends with a sense of progress—knowing that the science I 
worked on today isn't just for a paper, but for a product that will eventually go into a patient.
